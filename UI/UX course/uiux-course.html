Phase 1

The Foundations

Before touching any design tool, you need to understand what design actually is and how people think. This phase is the mental model that makes everything else click.

Lesson 1.1

UI vs UX ‚Äî What's the actual difference?

Beginner ‚è± 8 min read

This is the most confused topic in design. Most people use UI and UX interchangeably, but they describe fundamentally different things. Understanding the distinction is your first superpower.

üñ•Ô∏è
UI ‚Äî User Interface
The visual layer. Everything you can see and touch: buttons, colors, fonts, icons, spacing, animations. It's the "what does it look like and feel like?"
üß†
UX ‚Äî User Experience
The structural layer. How easy it is to achieve a goal. Navigation flow, logic, task completion. It's the "can I actually do what I came to do?"
üí° The Best Analogy Think of a house. UX is the floor plan ‚Äî where the rooms are, how you move between them. UI is the interior design ‚Äî the furniture, paint, lighting, decor. A beautiful house with a terrible floor plan is frustrating to live in. A well-planned house that looks ugly makes no one want to stay.
A product can have great UI but terrible UX (gorgeous app you can't figure out how to use)
A product can have poor UI but great UX (ugly but incredibly easy to use)
Your job is to nail both ‚Äî but always UX first, then UI
In small companies, one person often does both (UI/UX designer)
At large companies, these can be separate roles (UX researcher, Visual designer, Interaction designer)
"Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works." ‚Äî Steve Jobs
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 1.1 ‚Äî Train Your Eye

Open 3 apps on your phone (one you love, one you hate, one you're neutral about). For each app, write down:

  • One thing that's visually good or bad (UI)
  • One thing that's easy or confusing to use (UX)
  • Which of the two needs more work?

This exercise trains you to separate visual quality from usability ‚Äî a skill most junior designers lack.

Lesson 1.2

How Users Actually Think

Beginner ‚è± 10 min read

Users don't read ‚Äî they scan. They don't make decisions rationally ‚Äî they're lazy, distracted, and they trust their gut. The sooner you accept this, the better your designs will become.

üëÅÔ∏è
F-Pattern & Z-Pattern
Eye-tracking studies show users scan pages in an F or Z pattern ‚Äî hitting the top, then the left side. Place the most important information there.
‚ö°
Hick's Law
More choices = more time to decide = more frustration. When designing menus, forms, or options ‚Äî fewer is almost always better.
üß©
Cognitive Load
The brain has limited processing capacity. Every element you add makes the user work harder. Your job: reduce cognitive load at every turn.
üéØ
Fitts's Law
The time to click a target depends on its size and distance. Make important buttons BIG and easy to reach ‚Äî especially on mobile (thumb zones).
‚úÖ Practical Takeaway Never assume users will read your instructions or labels. Design as if every user is in a hurry, slightly distracted, and has never seen your product before. Because most of them are, and haven't.
Users satisfice ‚Äî they pick the first option that's "good enough," not the best one
Users follow conventions ‚Äî deviate from them only if you have a very good reason
Error prevention beats error recovery ‚Äî design so mistakes can't happen
Recognition over recall ‚Äî always show users what they can do, don't make them remember
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 1.2 ‚Äî Cognitive Load Audit

Pick any settings screen in an app. Count every piece of text, icon, and control on the screen. Then ask: which of these items are truly necessary? Could any be removed, combined, or hidden behind progressive disclosure?

Lesson 1.3

The Design Process Overview

Beginner ‚è± 8 min read

Professional designers don't sit down and immediately design a beautiful screen. They follow a process. Here's the process you'll use in every job, every project, for the rest of your career.

1. Discover (Research)
Understand the problem before trying to solve it. Who are the users? What do they need? What pain points exist? Talk to real users, collect data.
2. Define (Frame the Problem)
Synthesize your research into a clear problem statement. "Users need a way to X because Y." This is your design brief.
3. Ideate (Brainstorm)
Generate as many solutions as possible without judging them. Sketching, crazy eights, mind maps. Quantity over quality at this stage.
4. Prototype (Build a Draft)
Create a low-cost, testable version of your best idea. Start rough (paper/wireframe), get more polished as you validate.
5. Test (Get Feedback)
Put your prototype in front of real users and watch what happens. What confuses them? Where do they get stuck?
6. Iterate (Refine)
Take what you learned from testing and improve your design. Repeat steps 3‚Äì6 until it works well. This loop never fully ends.
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Beginner Mistake Jumping straight to Figma without doing steps 1‚Äì3. You end up designing beautiful solutions to the wrong problem. Research first, always.
Lesson 1.4

Design Thinking ‚Äî The Designer's Mindset

Beginner ‚è± 7 min read

Design Thinking is more than a process ‚Äî it's a way of approaching any problem. It's what separates designers who build what users need from those who just build what looks cool.

Empathy first: You are not your user. Suspend your assumptions and genuinely try to understand others' perspectives
Embrace ambiguity: Most design problems don't have one correct answer. Get comfortable with "it depends"
Fail fast, learn faster: A prototype that reveals a flaw in 2 days is better than a polished design that fails after 3 months
Radical collaboration: Great design rarely comes from one person. Learn to accept critique and build on others' ideas
Always ask "why": When you get a design request, ask why five times until you find the real underlying need
üìñ Real Example Client says: "Add a search bar to our homepage." You ask why. They say "users can't find products." You ask why again ‚Äî turns out users are landing on the wrong page from Google Ads. The real fix is a better landing page, not a search bar. This is design thinking in action.
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 1.4 ‚Äî The 5 Whys

Pick a frustrating experience you've had with any app. Write it down. Then ask "why does this problem exist?" 5 times, each time going deeper. By the 5th why, you'll usually find the real root cause ‚Äî and that's what a designer should fix.

Phase 2

Visual Design

This is the "makes it look good" phase ‚Äî but there's rigorous science behind every choice. Learn the principles that professional designers use to create interfaces that feel polished and purposeful.

Lesson 2.1

Color Theory for UI Designers

Beginner ‚è± 12 min read

Color is the most powerful and most misused tool in a designer's kit. It carries meaning, guides attention, conveys hierarchy, and triggers emotion. Here's how to use it intentionally.

60-30-10 Rule: 60% dominant color (backgrounds), 30% secondary (surfaces, cards), 10% accent (CTAs, highlights)
Limit your palette: Most great UI products use 2-3 core colors + neutrals. More colors = visual chaos
Semantic colors: Use consistent color for meaning ‚Äî green for success, red for error, yellow for warning, blue for info
Contrast ratio: Text needs at least 4.5:1 contrast against its background (WCAG AA standard) ‚Äî check every time

Example color system ‚Äî a modern dark interface palette:

Background
Surface
Accent
Highlight
Success
üõ†Ô∏è Tool Tip Use Coolors.co to generate palettes and Colour Contrast Checker (colourcontrast.cc) to verify accessibility. Never guess at contrast ‚Äî always check.
Warm colors (red, orange, yellow) energize and draw attention ‚Äî use for CTAs and alerts
Cool colors (blue, purple, teal) convey trust and calm ‚Äî common in finance, health, and productivity apps
Neutrals are the foundation of every great UI ‚Äî master shades of grey before anything else
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 2.1 ‚Äî Build a Color System

Go to Figma (free) and create a color system with:

  • A background color (very dark or very light)
  • A surface color (slightly lighter/darker than background)
  • One primary accent color
  • Semantic colors: error (red), success (green), warning (yellow)
  • At least 4 neutral greys for text hierarchy
Lesson 2.2

Typography ‚Äî The Soul of UI Design

Beginner ‚è± 10 min read

Typography is responsible for ~95% of design. Everything in a UI is text or relates to text. Getting typography right transforms mediocre work into professional work instantly.

Display Heading
Section Title / H2
Card Title / H3
Body text ‚Äî the workhorse of your type system. Comfortable line-height (1.5‚Äì1.7x), 14‚Äì16px for most UIs, generous enough to breathe.
Caption / Helper text ‚Äî smaller, muted, used for supporting context and metadata
Type scale: Stick to a consistent scale ‚Äî try 12, 14, 16, 20, 24, 32, 48px. Don't use arbitrary sizes
Line height: Body text needs 1.5‚Äì1.7x line height. Headings can be tighter at 1.1‚Äì1.2x
Font pairing: Max 2 fonts per project. One display/heading font, one readable body font
Weight hierarchy: Use weight to show importance ‚Äî 400 (regular) for body, 600-700 for titles, 800 for display
Never use all caps for long text ‚Äî it destroys readability. Use it only for small labels and tags
Line length: Ideal readable width is 60‚Äì80 characters per line. Wider or narrower hurts reading flow
üéì Free Font Resources Google Fonts (fonts.google.com) is your best free source. Great pairings: Syne + DM Sans, Playfair Display + Source Sans, Space Mono + Inter.
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 2.2 ‚Äî Build a Type System

In Figma, create a type scale using one font family. Define: Display, H1, H2, H3, Body Large, Body Regular, Caption, Label. Apply size, weight, line-height, and color for each. Save them as Text Styles.

Lesson 2.3

Spacing, Layout & The Grid

Intermediate ‚è± 10 min read

Spacing is one of the most underrated skills in design. Beginners under-space everything. Seniors know that generous, consistent spacing makes interfaces feel premium and professional.

8pt Grid System: All spacing should be multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64px). This creates mathematical harmony
Related items close together: Use proximity ‚Äî elements that belong together should be visually grouped
Breathing room: White space is NOT wasted space. It makes content legible and interfaces feel premium
Consistent padding: Use the same padding inside components throughout your design
12-column grid: Most web layouts use a 12-column grid ‚Äî it divides into 2, 3, 4, and 6 evenly
‚ö†Ô∏è The Cramping Trap The #1 sign of a junior designer: everything is too close together, margins are tiny, and the whole page feels suffocating. When in doubt ‚Äî add more space. You can always tighten it later.
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 2.3 ‚Äî Spacing Audit

Take any design you've made (or a screenshot of any app). Measure the spacing between elements. Are they consistent? Are they on the 8pt grid? Redesign one section with intentional, generous spacing and compare the two versions.

Lesson 2.4

Visual Hierarchy ‚Äî Guiding the Eye

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

Visual hierarchy is your ability to guide users through a design in the order you intend. Without it, users don't know where to look ‚Äî and they leave. With it, they flow naturally toward the actions you want them to take.

üî§
Size
Bigger = more important. Headlines are bigger than body text. Primary actions are bigger than secondary ones.
üé®
Color & Contrast
High-contrast elements attract attention first. Use your accent color sparingly ‚Äî it should mark only the most important elements.
‚öñÔ∏è
Weight
Bold text reads before regular text. Use font weight to signal importance without changing size.
üìç
Position
Top-left gets attention first (in LTR languages). Place the most critical information where the eye naturally lands.
Lesson 2.5

Gestalt Principles in UI

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

Gestalt is the psychological theory behind how humans perceive visual elements. These principles are baked into every great interface design ‚Äî whether the designer knew it or not. Learn them consciously.

Proximity: Elements close together appear related. Use consistent spacing to group related UI elements
Similarity: Elements that look alike are perceived as a group. Use consistent styling for the same type of component
Continuation: The eye follows lines, curves, and patterns. Use alignment to guide attention across the screen
Closure: The brain fills in gaps to complete shapes. Partial borders, open containers, and implied grids all work because of closure
Figure/Ground: We naturally separate what we're looking at from the background. Cards, modals, and tooltips all rely on this
üì± Real Example When you see a navigation bar at the bottom of an app with icons spaced equally, your brain groups them into one "navigation" unit (proximity + similarity). When one icon is highlighted, your brain knows it's the current state (similarity broken intentionally). All Gestalt in action.
Phase 3

UX Fundamentals

This is where you learn to think about your user's entire journey ‚Äî not just a single screen. UX is strategy. It's the backbone of every decision you'll make as a designer.

Lesson 3.1

User Research ‚Äî Know Before You Design

Intermediate ‚è± 12 min read

User research is how you replace assumptions with evidence. Without it, you're designing for yourself. With it, you're designing for the people who will actually use it.

üí¨
User Interviews
1-on-1 conversations to understand motivations, goals, and frustrations. Ask open-ended questions. Listen 80% of the time. Don't pitch your solution.
üëÅÔ∏è
Contextual Inquiry
Watch users in their natural environment using the product (or competitor's product). What you observe is often different from what they tell you.
üìä
Surveys
Quantitative research at scale. Great for validating hypotheses. Bad for discovering new insights. Combine with qualitative methods.
üîç
Competitive Analysis
Study how competitors solve the same problems. Identify patterns (don't break them unless you have a very good reason) and unmet needs.
üí° Interview Golden Rules Never ask leading questions ("Don't you think it's confusing?"). Ask instead: "Tell me about the last time you tried to do X." Never mention your solution during research ‚Äî you'll bias the answers. Ask about behavior, not opinions.
Lesson 3.2

Personas ‚Äî Designing for Real People

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

A persona is a fictional but research-based character that represents a segment of your users. It keeps your team aligned around who you're designing for ‚Äî especially when debates arise.

üë§ Example Persona Amara, 28 ‚Äî Marketing Manager in Nairobi
Tech-comfortable but time-poor. Uses apps on the go on a 4G connection. Frustrated by apps that require too many steps. Goal: quickly track campaign performance without opening a laptop. Pain point: dashboards with too much data, unclear what to act on. Quote: "Just tell me what's important."
Include: name, age, job, technical comfort level, goals, frustrations, and a real quote from research
Base personas on patterns from multiple real user interviews ‚Äî never make them up
Keep 2‚Äì4 primary personas. More than that becomes unmanageable
Refer back to your persona whenever you make a design decision: "Would Amara find this intuitive?"
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 3.2 ‚Äî Create a Persona

Interview 2-3 people about how they use any app (food delivery, banking, social media). Find common patterns in their answers and create one persona based on that data. Include a photo from Unsplash, a name, and their goals, frustrations, and one quote.

Lesson 3.3

User Flows & Journey Maps

Intermediate ‚è± 10 min read

Before designing a single screen, map out the path your user takes. A user flow shows every step from entry point to goal completion. A journey map adds emotion and context to that path.

User Flow: A diagram showing every screen and decision point a user goes through to complete a task (e.g., checkout flow, onboarding flow)
Task Flow: A simplified flow for one specific task ‚Äî no branching, just the happy path
Journey Map: A wider view ‚Äî shows awareness ‚Üí consideration ‚Üí use ‚Üí loyalty stages, with emotions at each stage
Use simple shapes: rectangles for screens, diamonds for decisions, ovals for start/end points
Always design the flow before the screens. Screen design is easier when you know the full journey
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 3.3 ‚Äî Map a User Flow

Pick a simple task: "User signs up and completes their first action in a to-do app." Map every screen and decision in that flow using FigJam or any flowchart tool. How many steps does it take? Can you reduce any?

Lesson 3.4

Information Architecture

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

Information Architecture (IA) is how content is organized and navigated. It's the skeleton of any digital product. Good IA is invisible ‚Äî users just "know" where things are. Bad IA makes users feel lost.

Card Sorting: A research technique where users group content cards by how they think they relate ‚Äî use this to design navigation menus
Sitemap: A diagram of all the pages in a product and how they connect hierarchically
Navigation patterns: Tab bars (mobile), hamburger menus, top navs, sidebars ‚Äî choose based on content volume and usage frequency
Mental models: Design your IA to match how users already think about the world, not how your database is structured
Lesson 3.5

Wireframing ‚Äî Think Before You Polish

Intermediate ‚è± 10 min read

Wireframes are low-fidelity blueprints of screens. They let you solve layout and UX problems quickly ‚Äî before investing time in visual design. The uglier the wireframe, the better the feedback (people critique structure, not aesthetics).

Example: Landing Page Wireframe

Hero: Headline + CTA + Image
Low-fi first: Start with pencil/paper or very rough Figma boxes. Save the detail for later
Annotate everything: Add notes explaining what happens on click, hover, or submission
Focus on layout, hierarchy, and flow ‚Äî ignore color, fonts, and real images at this stage
Test your wireframes with users before going hi-fi. It's 10x cheaper to fix problems here
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 3.5 ‚Äî Wireframe a Full Flow

Wireframe a 4-screen mobile onboarding flow for a budgeting app. Screens: Welcome ‚Üí Sign Up ‚Üí Set Monthly Budget ‚Üí Dashboard. Use only grey boxes, simple text labels, and arrows between screens.

Lesson 4.2

Figma Fundamentals ‚Äî The Core Skills

Intermediate ‚è± 15 min read

These are the Figma skills every junior designer needs before applying for a job. Work through each one in Figma as you read.

Frames vs Groups: Always use Frames (F) for screens and containers. Groups for temporary collections. Frames have constraints; groups don't
Auto Layout: The most important Figma feature. Makes your designs responsive and easy to update. Apply it to buttons, cards, lists, everything
Constraints: Tells elements how to behave when their parent frame resizes. Essential for responsive design
Styles: Save your colors and text styles as named Styles. Change once, update everywhere
Components: Create reusable UI elements. Change the main component and every instance updates automatically
Layers panel: Name every layer clearly (not "Frame 4743"). Good layer structure = professional designer
Keyboard shortcuts: F (frame), R (rectangle), T (text), A (select children), Ctrl+G (group), Ctrl+D (duplicate) ‚Äî learn these cold
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes in Figma Not using Auto Layout (everything breaks when content changes), using Groups instead of Frames, not naming layers, ignoring the Inspect panel (developers use this). Fix these habits early.
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 4.2 ‚Äî Build a Button Component

In Figma, create a Button component with 4 variants: Primary, Secondary, Disabled, and Loading state. Use Auto Layout so the button resizes with its text. Save the fill color as a Color Style. Test that changing the main component updates all instances.

Lesson 4.3

Components & Variants ‚Äî Design at Scale

Intermediate ‚è± 10 min read

Variants let you create all states of a component in one place ‚Äî like a button in default, hover, pressed, disabled, and loading states. This is how professional teams work.

Use the Component Properties panel to define Variants ‚Äî each property becomes a dropdown for your team
Name variants with clear descriptors: Type=Primary/Secondary, Size=Small/Medium/Large, State=Default/Hover/Disabled
Always build components before designing screens ‚Äî it forces you to think systematically
Think in systems: don't just make one card ‚Äî make a Card component that works for all card types in your app
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 4.3 ‚Äî Build a Card Component

Create a Card component with these variants: Type (Default/Featured), With/Without Image, State (Default/Hover/Selected). Make it work with Auto Layout. Test it by placing 6 cards in a grid and changing their variants.

Lesson 4.4

Prototyping in Figma

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

Prototypes turn static screens into interactive experiences that you can test with real users. You don't need to code. You just connect screens and add interactions in Figma's Prototype mode.

Use the Prototype panel to draw connections between frames (click button ‚Üí navigate to next screen)
Add Smart Animate transitions between screens for polished, realistic feel
Use Interactive Components for in-component interactions (checkbox toggle, dropdown open/close)
Share the prototype link with users for testing ‚Äî they can interact on any device, no install needed
Prototype fidelity should match the test goal: low-fi for layout testing, high-fi for visual/interaction testing
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 4.4 ‚Äî Your First Prototype

Take the 4-screen wireframe from Exercise 3.5. Build it in Figma, add visual styling, then connect all 4 screens in Prototype mode with transitions. Share the link and test it on your phone. Does the flow feel intuitive?

Phase 5

Design Process & Testing

Design isn't done when it looks good ‚Äî it's done when it works well for users. This phase teaches you how to validate, iterate, and design inclusively.

Lesson 5.1

Usability Testing ‚Äî Let Users Break Your Design

Intermediate ‚è± 10 min read

Usability testing is the single highest-ROI activity in product design. 5 users will reveal 85% of your usability problems. You don't need expensive labs ‚Äî a screen share call works perfectly.

Give tasks, not guidance: "Try to add a product to your cart" ‚Äî not "click the button in the top right"
Think-aloud protocol: Ask users to narrate what they're thinking. Silence often means confusion
Never defend your design: When a user struggles, it's data. Don't explain or apologize ‚Äî just observe and note
Look for patterns: One user's confusion might be noise. Three users hitting the same problem is a signal
Test early: Test wireframes, not just polished designs. Catching problems early is cheaper and less emotionally painful
üìã Test Script Template 1. "Thanks for joining. I'm testing the design, not you ‚Äî there are no wrong answers." 2. "Please think out loud as you go." 3. Task: "Imagine you just installed this app. Please [task]." 4. Follow-up: "What did you expect to happen?" 5. "On a scale of 1-10, how easy was that?"
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 5.1 ‚Äî Run a Guerrilla Test

Share your Figma prototype from Exercise 4.4 with 3 people (friends, family). Give them one task and watch silently. Note every moment they pause, hesitate, or click the wrong thing. After testing, identify the 2 biggest usability problems and fix them.

Lesson 5.2

Iteration ‚Äî Design is Never "Done"

Intermediate ‚è± 8 min read

Professional designers are not attached to their work. Every design is a hypothesis. Testing is how you find out if your hypothesis was right. Iteration is how you make it better.

After testing, prioritize issues by severity: critical (blocks task completion) ‚Üí serious (causes frustration) ‚Üí minor (cosmetic)
Fix critical issues first before re-testing. Don't try to fix everything at once
Track versions ‚Äî Figma's version history is your safety net. Use it. Name your versions
Sometimes the right answer is to go back to a flow diagram and rethink the structure, not just tweak the UI
Feedback from developers and stakeholders is also input for iteration ‚Äî not just user testing
Lesson 5.3

Accessibility ‚Äî Design for Everyone

Advanced ‚è± 12 min read

Accessibility (a11y) isn't optional ‚Äî it's a legal requirement in many countries and a moral obligation. 15% of the world lives with some form of disability. Design for them, and you improve the experience for everyone.

Color contrast: 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (WCAG AA). Never use color as the only indicator of meaning
Touch targets: Minimum 44x44px for interactive elements (Apple HIG standard). Fingers are not precise
Focus states: Every interactive element must have a visible focus state for keyboard users
Alt text: Every meaningful image needs descriptive alt text. Decorative images can have empty alt=""
Don't rely on color alone: Use icons + labels, patterns, or text alongside color coding
Readable font sizes: Minimum 16px for body text. Never use 10‚Äì11px for anything users need to read
üõ†Ô∏è Accessibility Tools Use the Stark plugin in Figma to check contrast ratios and simulate color blindness. Use Accessibility Insights (free from Microsoft) to audit live websites.
Lesson 5.4

Responsive Design ‚Äî Mobile, Tablet, Desktop

Advanced ‚è± 10 min read

Your designs will be used on screens of every size. Responsive design is how you ensure the experience is great everywhere ‚Äî not just on the device you designed on.

Mobile first: Start designing for the smallest screen. It forces you to prioritize. Then scale up to tablet and desktop
Common breakpoints: Mobile (375px), Tablet (768px), Desktop (1280px+). Design at least mobile and desktop
Stacking vs side-by-side: Multi-column layouts on desktop typically stack vertically on mobile
Touch vs mouse: Mobile users need larger tap targets; desktop users have precision cursors. Design accordingly
Navigation patterns shift: Desktop nav bars become bottom tab bars or hamburger menus on mobile
üèãÔ∏è Exercise 5.4 ‚Äî Responsive Redesign

Take any of your previous design screens and design its mobile AND desktop version. They should solve the same goal but with layouts appropriate to each context. Focus on navigation, spacing, and content hierarchy.

Phase 6

Design Systems

Design Systems are how real product teams stay consistent at scale. Understanding them separates junior designers from mid-senior ones. This is where you start thinking like a design engineer.

Lesson 6.1

What is a Design System?

Advanced ‚è± 8 min read

A design system is a collection of reusable components, guided by clear standards, that enables teams to build consistent products faster. Think of it as the single source of truth for a product's visual and interactive language.

A design system includes: design tokens (variables), a component library, documentation, patterns, and guidelines
Famous examples: Google's Material Design, Apple's Human Interface Guidelines, IBM Carbon, Atlassian Design System
Benefits: consistency across the product, faster design (no reinventing the wheel), cleaner dev handoff, easier onboarding
Knowing how to use AND contribute to a design system is a job requirement at most mid-to-large companies
üìñ Study These Free Design Systems Material Design 3 (material.io) ‚Äî Google's system, excellent documentation. Carbon Design System (carbondesignsystem.com) ‚Äî IBM's enterprise system. Atlassian (atlassian.design) ‚Äî great for SaaS products. Studying these teaches you how to think systematically.
Lesson 6.2

Building a Style Guide

Advanced ‚è± 8 min read

A style guide documents the visual foundations of your system: colors, typography, spacing, and icons. It's the first thing you build in a design system.

Component Libraries

A component library is a collection of UI building blocks ‚Äî buttons, inputs, cards, modals, navigation elements ‚Äî built as Figma components, ready to use across any screen in your product.

Lesson 6.4

Design Tokens

Advanced ‚è± 8 min read

Design tokens are named variables that store visual values like colors, spacing, and typography. They're the bridge between design and code ‚Äî allowing designers and developers to speak the same language.

Instead of hardcoding #7c5cfc, you use a token: `color.accent.primary` ‚Äî the code and design reference the same name
Tokens enable theming ‚Äî swap all token values to switch between a light and dark theme instantly
In Figma, use Variables (the new feature) to create tokens for colors, spacing, and typography
Tokens follow a hierarchy: Global ‚Üí Semantic ‚Üí Component. E.g., blue-500 ‚Üí color.interactive.primary ‚Üí button.background

Lesson 7.2

Writing Compelling Case Studies

Advanced ‚è± 10 min read

A case study is not a gallery of your best screens. It's a story about how you solved a problem. The structure below works every single time.

1. The Problem (Hook)
"Users were abandoning checkout at a 68% rate on mobile. Here's how I figured out why and fixed it." Start with impact, not process.
2. Research & Discovery
What you did to understand the problem. User interviews, data analysis, competitive research. Show artifacts (interview quotes, data charts, personas).
3. Problem Definition
What insight did your research surface? What's the real problem to solve? Write your "How Might We" statement.
4. Ideation & Decision Making
Show your thinking ‚Äî early sketches, options you considered, and WHY you chose the direction you did.
5. Design & Iteration
Show the progression from wireframes to final designs. Highlight what changed after user testing and why.
6. Outcome & Reflection
What happened? What would you do differently? Showing self-awareness is a green flag for hiring managers.
Lesson 7.3

Interview Prep ‚Äî What They'll Actually Ask

Advanced ‚è± 10 min read

Design interviews typically have 3 parts: portfolio walkthrough, design challenge, and behavioral questions. Here's how to ace each one.

Portfolio walkthrough: Practice telling your case study as a 5-minute verbal story. Focus on decisions and tradeoffs ‚Äî not features
Design challenge: You'll be given a prompt like "Design an app that helps elderly users take their medications." Ask clarifying questions first. Sketch on paper or whiteboard. Narrate your thinking out loud
Critique exercise: They show you a screen and ask "what would you improve?" Start with what works, then what you'd change and why (use design principles, not personal taste)
üí¨ Common Interview Questions "Walk me through your design process." / "Tell me about a design decision you made that you later changed. Why?" / "How do you handle stakeholder feedback that you disagree with?" / "How do you know when a design is good enough?"
Always ask clarifying questions before jumping into a design challenge ‚Äî this is expected and shows maturity
Think out loud during design exercises. Your reasoning matters as much as the output
Have specific examples ready for behavioral questions using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
